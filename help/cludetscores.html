The measure of Importance is described in:
  • WIELING, Martijn & John NERBONNE (to appear)
    ‘Bipartite spectral graph partitioning for clustering dialect varieties and detecting their linguistic features’. Computer Speech and Language. doi:10.1016/j.csl.2010.05.004
    Available online (http://www.sciencedirect.com/) since May 21, 2010.
The use of Importance in Gabmap necessarily deviates from the use in the above cited paper. The paper applies Importance to data that consists of places and sound correspondences. In Gabmap our data consists of responses and variants, where each unique variant can represent one or more linguistic features.

The second author of the paper allows that variants can be used instead of sound correspondences, but insists that the formula for Importance can only be applied to data of places. (Personal communication.)

So, instead of evaluating the raw data, we must reduce the data over places, into one group of places where a wanted variant occurs (for each place, this can be sometimes or always), and another group of places where no such variant occurs (whether or not we even have any data for those places).

This method does not work well. In fact, a better method is available in Gabmap. For who is interested, contact support@gabmap.nl

The problem

In Gabmap's cluster determinants, we select a linguistic item, we draw a set of word patterns from it, and score it for its match to a predefined geographic region. Then we take another linguistic item, draw a set of word patterns, and score it for its match with the same predefined geographic region.

We cannot compare these two scores, because they are of draws from different data collections. Those data collections can at times be very different from each other in the way they cover the geographic region.

Some answers

There are two ways to deal with the problem.
  1. Use a method for scoring that is reasonably robust in this situation, and just analyse the data as it is. In Gabmap we don't need an accurate score. We only need a score that is good enough to point us to linguistic items that merit further inspection.
  2. You can "map" the data onto the map, so instead of responses with some features, we have locations with some features. The locations are identical.
Number 1 works quite effectively, as experiments in Gabmap have shown. However, it is not the way it is currently done.

Number 2 is the way it is now done in Gabmap. But the method for mapping is very crude. As a result, the scores don't help us find interesting features as well as they should.

What is needed is an accurate way to map data onto the map. First, we would have to use per location ratios of occurrences of desired patterns, instead of a binary 1 (it occurs once or more) or 0 (it does not occur). Second, we need to give a reasonable estimate for locations without data, by interpolating, based on geographic distribution, from locations that do have data. A best guess. Currently, this is not the way it is done in Gabmap.

Needs rewriting to make it understandable for the average user.

This methods works on the individual responses in the data. The result may be that we are comparing scores for items based on data that covers quite different areas, due to missing data. So, the scores may perhaps not be comparable. This may lead to a suboptimal ranking of the items. It doesn't effect the selection of patterns within an item. (At least, not with the F score. The Importance score suffers notably, because of the variable asymmetry inherent in the method.)

Is this a problem? You don't need the values to be accurate. All you need is a sensible ranking. You may get some high-ranking items that don't lead you to a clear, determining feature. But this may happen even in the accurate method, perhaps not as often. If it happens, just move on to the next item.

The results for items with very few data are less reliable than those for items with many data. So we prevent items with very few data to get scores as extreme as those for items with many data. We do this by adding 1 to each count of true positives, false positives, false negatives, and true negatives. (The last one isn't used in the F score.) This method is based on the predictive values of small sample draws from large data sets.

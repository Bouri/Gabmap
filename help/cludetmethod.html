Currently, Gabmap implements two cluster determinant methods. Cluster determinants try to find items that are characteristic of a given cluster (set of sites).

  • The first (recommended) cluster determinant, labeled "Distance based" implements the method described in
    Jelena Prokić, Çağrı Çöltekin & John Nerbonne (2012). Detecting Shibboleths. In: M. Butt & J. Prokić (eds.) Visualization of Language Patters and Uncovering Language History from Multilingual Resources. Workshop at the 13th Conference of the European Chapter of the Association for computational Linguistics. Avignon, France. 2012. pp.72-80.
    the method is based on finding items that are uniform within the cluster of interest, while different from the rest of the sites. The two components within difference and between difference characterize the average difference within the sites in the cluster, and the average difference between the sites in the cluster and the rest of the sites in the data set with respect to given item. Items whose within difference is low, and between difference is high are regarded as characteristic items, shibboleths. Since the raw differences values differ based on different data sets, they are normalized so that the differences are comparable across different data sets. The aggregate score primarily used for sorting the items is simply between_score - within_score. (In earlier versions of Gabmap, an aggregate score based on geometric mean between these two quantities are used such that the score is the geometric mean of these two quantities if within difference is negative and between difference positive, `0' otherwise.)

    Missing values
    Calculation of differences between individual pairs of sites may fail, typically, due to missing values (e.g., a pronunciation that has not been recorded in a particular site). This may affect the scale and the reliability of the average distance calculations used for this method. The option "Missing values" allows you to specify how to treat missing values. A number between 0 and 1 is interpreted as the minimum ratio of missing data allowed. While a number greater than one indicates the absolute number of missing values allowed. If the ratio or number of missing values is greater than the specified limit the score is will show 'NA'. Otherwise, the average distances will be calculated ignoring the missing values.

  • The second method labeled "Categorical" is loosely based on
    Martijn Wieling and John Nerbonne (2011). Bipartite spectral graph partitioning for clustering dialect varieties and detecting their linguistic features. Computer Speech and Language 25, 2011. 700-715.
